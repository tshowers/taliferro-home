Introduction

I've been an IT Consultant for over ten years. In that time, I learned that everyone wants to be promoted at work. It's natural to want recognition, and it's also a good idea to want more money. But more than anything else, what my clients crave is the feeling of being appreciated by their superiors—and when their coworkers (and even clients) look up to them as an expert in their field. In other words, they want people to think they are fantastic. And if you can help them get there? Not only will your client feel great about themselves, but so will you. Now here's how you can help your client get promoted.

Give them something to be promoted for

The easiest way to get your clients a promotion is to help them with a project that they are passionate about. This will be the most effective and rewarding way for them to demonstrate their skills and abilities, leading to the ability to earn more money and make a more significant impact on their organization. If you can find out what they want to do in the future, even better. Do they want to be promoted? What would it take? Is an open position available at higher levels in their company that might be a good fit for them? Find out what those things are so that you can make sure that when people look at your client's resume or application for another job, all of these things come together nicely.

Help them build a case for their promotion.

Before you start helping your client get promoted, ensure you have all the information needed to build a strong case for them. If they don't have enough experience and knowledge to do their job well, then focus on getting them some experience and knowledge first. This will help them learn more about what is expected from them in their role and how they can improve over time.

Next, consider what makes a good case for someone else's promotion. Should this person be paid more because they are great at communicating with customers? Are there any awards or other accolades that show this person's exceptionality? What skills does this person possess that would benefit their organization if used more often? Answering these questions will allow you to present evidence showing why your client deserves a promotion based on their ability rather than just telling people who will decide whether or not they get what they want to hear (e.g., "this employee has been loyal").

Help them get noticed

Now that you know what they want, it's time to ask for it. The key to making this process work is being specific about the request; don't just tell someone "I want them to get promoted." Instead, focus on the details: "I want them to get promoted by X date and be given Y title/role/responsibility. If I could also get Z in there too, that would be fantastic!"

Remember that this is a collaborative project between you and your client—you should agree on what success looks like before starting down any path together.

If all goes according to plan with this strategy, then congratulations! However, if things don't go as smoothly or aren't possible at this time due to external factors outside both parties' control, don't worry: There's always next year. Your client will have achieved their professional goals faster than expected, thanks largely because of your efforts on their behalf.

Help them build relationships with the right people

To help your client get promoted, you can help them build relationships with people who can help them.

It sounds obvious - but it's not always as easy as it sounds. People who are good at networking know how to make friends and build relationships with the right people. They know what to say, how to be authentic and genuine in their interactions, and they understand that building strong relationships takes time and effort. If your client is new to networking, or if they're struggling to see why it matters (or even don't quite feel comfortable doing it yet), here are some tips for helping them on their way:

Help them understand the importance of networking and why it will help them achieve their goals. For example: "Networking isn't just about getting new business; it's also about building stronger connections with other professionals who share similar interests." Or "Networking helps you learn from others' experiences." Or "Networking leads directly into job opportunities."

Explain how important it is for them to have a wide range of contacts in different fields so that when an opportunity arises outside their current area of expertise, they'll have someone ready at hand whom they can ask for advice or support with information gathering/researching etc.

Talk about their future career goals

The best way to help your client is to discuss their future career goals. Ask them what they want to do in the future and if there are any areas where they'd like to develop more skills or experience. You can also talk about what kind of salary or title they would like in the next year, five years, or ten years from now.

Be honest about their chances

Before you start talking about how to get your client a promotion, it's essential, to be honest about their chances. If you think they have a chance, tell them. But if you don't think they have an opportunity, or even if they do, but they don't realize it, tell them that too. The more information they have going into the interview process, the better off they'll be—and in turn, so will your relationship with them.

Sometimes getting your client a promotion is as simple as helping them do their job better.

It's best to start by asking what they want to achieve and how they envision doing it. If they tell you that they want to get promoted but can't articulate what value they will add or how their skills and experience make them the best candidate, there isn't much we can do here other than offer encouragement and support. But if the client has done some research and comes up with an action plan on how they can demonstrate their value to get promoted, then that's something we can work with.

For example: let's say your client has been working in accounting for 3 years but wants to move into marketing so she can learn about consumer trends and social media—and eventually become a brand ambassador for her company at industry events. She believes these transferable skills will allow her more advancement opportunities later. What does this mean? It means she needs an opportunity where she has the chance (1) demonstrate her ability with those soft skills; (2) show off her new knowledge of consumer behavior; and (3) put those two things together into some kind of coherent pitch that showcases how she could be valuable in this new role.

Conclusion

As you can see, getting your client a promotion isn't always as simple as writing them a letter or making a phone call. But it should be possible if they work hard and do their job well. And if not, well, then maybe they don't deserve one anyway.
