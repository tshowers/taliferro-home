Introduction

People don't want to be sold, but the good news is that there's always a way around that. There are ways to approach someone even though they don't know you that can help you form a connection. A prospect is more likely to listen when you have something important to say. This article will look at strategies for approaching strangers about your product or service in person and online.

Empathize with your prospect's point of view

To effectively approach a prospect, it's essential to put yourself in your prospect's shoes. You can ask questions and listen carefully to what they have to say. You can also show empathy by acknowledging that you understand their situation.

Focus on their benefits, not the features of your product or service

You might think that the best way to convince a customer to buy your product is by focusing on the features of your product or service. After all, everyone knows what a feature is—it's the "what" of the product: how it works, what it does, and how it can help them.

But when it comes down to it, benefits are much more important than features. Benefits speak to a customer's needs and want—they make them want to buy from you (and only you). That's why I recommend that when someone asks you about your business or offers suggestions for how they could use your products or services differently, focus on highlighting the benefits first.

Tell a story

The second thing you need to do is tell a story. Tell a story about how your product or service helped someone. Use real numbers and specific details, focusing on benefits rather than features.

A personal story can be beneficial. "I used this product/service to..." or "When I was doing X, I had problems with Y." Here's an example: "I didn't know what type of business I wanted to start until my Dad showed me his website building tool — it helped me get my site up and running so fast!"

You should use stories illustrating how you came up with your product/service idea in the first place — "I noticed that X happened in industry Y" or "I was trying to solve problem Z."

Don't bad mouth or slight the competition

You should avoid discussing your competition unless it's relevant to the conversation. For example, if someone mentions that they like working with another company and are interested in their services as well as yours, then go ahead and say how you're different from them. But if someone asks for help deciding between two available products (both of which are made by companies other than yours), don't say anything negative about either company. Instead of saying, "I think Product A is better than Product B," say something like, "Product A works differently than Product B. Feel free to check out both options."

Also, remember that this advice applies not only when you're selling a product or service, but anywhere you spend time with people—not just at work.

Help them see your vision for them

Once you clearly understand your product or service and its benefits, it's time for you to communicate it to the prospect.

What is your product or service? What are the benefits of using it? How does it fit in with their goals and ambitions? How does it help them achieve their goals?

Write a good subject line for an email, tweet, or message

When you're reaching out to someone you don't know, the subject line is one of the most critical parts of an email. It should be clear and concise and not have anything in it that will make a reader feel like they need to "unsubscribe" from your emails or avoid them altogether (like "Hey!" or "This thing happened!").

Like a tweet or other social media message, I think "Hi [name]!" is a good default for subject lines. This makes it personal—you're addressing this person by name, so they know who's contacting them—and friendly. You could also use something like: "You might be interested in…" or "My new product/service..." but avoid using generic phrases like these because they don't stand out as much as more personalized ones.

Get to the point quickly in face-to-face communication

It can be challenging to get your point across in one-on-one communication. It would help if you did it quickly and with conviction. Don't waste a prospect's time, and don't be afraid to ask for what you want.

  • Ask them directly if you can meet with them at their convenience, so they know when the meeting will take place. This is important because no one likes being put on the spot like this—they might not have time available, or they might have something else going on that day/weekend/month (like a family event or an out-of-town trip). Scheduling ahead of time gives people options that help keep things organized for both parties involved in setting up a meeting (and it also shows respect towards them).
  • Ask for what you want: Don't hesitate when asking strangers about business opportunities, such as working together on projects like web design projects, marketing campaigns, writing articles, etc. Get straight into talking about what product or service would benefit them best since every person has different needs related to these services.

Be polite and respectful when you approach a stranger

Be polite and concerned about their time when you ask if they have a moment to chat.

  • Ask if it's a good time to chat.
  • Be brief and to the point when explaining your product or service, making sure to mention what makes yours different from others on the market.
  • Don't be pushy or aggressive; if they say no thanks, then don't try again—there will be someone else who enjoys talking about their interests. You may have to wait for them to finish with something or pause what they're doing if they're in the middle of something important.

Share that their input is valuable to you, and clarify how much you'd like to hear their thoughts

The most important thing to remember when approaching a stranger is to be polite and kind. Be open, honest, and willing to listen. You want to ensure you are not being pushy or overly aggressive in your approach.

It's also vital that you share that their input is valuable to you and clarify how much you'd like to hear their thoughts. If the person is hesitant about talking about what they do for a living, try asking them about another topic instead of jumping straight into business talk.

At the end of your conversation (or earlier), ask if it's OK if you follow up with them once you've made some adjustments per their feedback.

When approaching a stranger about your product or service, you should have specific numbers in mind. If possible, try to learn the number of employees at their company, approximate revenue, etc. This shows that you've done your homework and want to tailor your offer for them specifically.

It's easy to find this information online, look up their name on LinkedIn, search for them on Google and then check out their company's website (or even call the phone number listed).

Take rejection graciously

  • Don't take it personally if they say no, especially if they give you feedback on how to improve your product or service
  • Thank them for their time.
  • Ask if it's OK to follow up with them later.
  • Be positive and professional! Don't badmouth the person or your product/service in any way; they may be a friend or family member of someone with influence over your prospects, so be polite and friendly even if you have been rejected.

There are ways to approach someone even though they don't know you that can help you form a connection

Tell a story. This will help them see themselves using your service and hopefully make them feel like they need it in their life.

Focus on their benefits, not the features of the product or service. Help them see your vision for them (and why it's better than what else is out there)

Conclusion

The bottom line is that people don't want to be sold; they want to be engaged. When you can create a connection with someone – whether through empathy or storytelling – your odds of getting them interested in what you're doing increases dramatically.

If you can do this while respecting their time and avoiding common pitfalls like badmouthing competitors, you'll be well on making more sales.
